# Intellectual Property & Employment Law Reference

## 1. Patent Law

### Patent Types
- **Utility patents**: Protect new and useful processes, machines, manufactures, or compositions of matter. Term: 20 years from earliest effective filing date. Subject to maintenance fees at 3.5, 7.5, and 11.5 years post-grant.
- **Design patents**: Protect new, original, and ornamental design for an article of manufacture. Term: 15 years from grant date (for applications filed after May 13, 2015). No maintenance fees required.
- **Plant patents**: Protect distinct and new varieties of asexually reproduced plants. Term: 20 years from filing date. No maintenance fees.

### Provisional Applications
- Establishes an early filing date without beginning the patent examination process.
- 12-month priority period: applicant must file a non-provisional application within 12 months or the provisional lapses.
- No formal claims required, but must satisfy written description and enablement for any claims later relying on the provisional date.
- Cannot claim priority to an earlier provisional (no daisy-chaining).
- Lower filing fees; useful for securing a filing date while assessing commercial viability.
- A provisional application is never examined and never matures into a patent on its own.

### Patentability Requirements
- **Novelty (35 U.S.C. 102)**: The invention must not be anticipated by a single prior art reference. Under the AIA (post-March 16, 2013), prior art includes anything patented, described in a printed publication, in public use, on sale, or otherwise available to the public before the effective filing date. One-year grace period for inventor's own disclosures.
- **Non-obviousness (35 U.S.C. 103)**: The invention must not be obvious to a person having ordinary skill in the art (PHOSITA) at the time of filing. Graham v. John Deere factors: (1) scope and content of prior art, (2) differences between prior art and claims, (3) level of ordinary skill, (4) secondary considerations (commercial success, long-felt need, failure of others, skepticism).
- **Utility (35 U.S.C. 101)**: The invention must have a specific, substantial, and credible utility. Rarely at issue except in chemical/biotech fields.
- **Enablement and Written Description (35 U.S.C. 112)**: The specification must enable a PHOSITA to make and use the invention without undue experimentation. Written description must show the inventor possessed the claimed invention at the time of filing. Claim definiteness required.

### Subject Matter Eligibility (Alice/Mayo Framework)
After Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank International (2014), the two-step test applies:
- **Step 1**: Are the claims directed to a judicial exception (abstract idea, law of nature, natural phenomenon)?
- **Step 2A (Prong 1)**: Does the claim recite a judicial exception?
- **Step 2A (Prong 2)**: If yes, does the claim integrate the exception into a practical application?
- **Step 2B**: If not integrated, does the claim recite an inventive concept (significantly more than the exception itself)?

Key judicial exceptions: fundamental economic practices, methods of organizing human activity, mathematical concepts, mental processes, laws of nature, natural phenomena. Software and business method patents are heavily scrutinized. Life sciences claims involving natural products must show markedly different characteristics.

### Patent Prosecution Overview
- Filing: non-provisional application with specification, claims, drawings, oath/declaration, fees.
- Examination: USPTO examiner reviews for patentability; issues office actions for rejections/objections.
- Response: applicant has typically 3 months (extendable to 6) to respond to office actions.
- Allowance: examiner issues Notice of Allowance; applicant pays issue fee.
- Continuations: file before parent issues to pursue additional claims on the same disclosure. Continuation-in-part (CIP) adds new matter. Divisional filed in response to restriction requirement.
- Request for Continued Examination (RCE): continues prosecution after final rejection.
- Post-grant proceedings: inter partes review (IPR), post-grant review (PGR), ex parte reexamination.

### Freedom to Operate (FTO) Analysis
- Identify relevant patents and published applications in the technology space.
- Analyze claims (not just abstracts/titles) of identified patents.
- Determine if the proposed product/process falls within the scope of any valid, enforceable claim.
- Assess patent validity (potential invalidity arguments reduce risk).
- Consider design-around options for high-risk patents.
- Document the analysis for business decision-making (note: FTO opinions can be discoverable).
- Engage patent counsel for formal opinions to establish good faith (can negate willfulness).

### Patent Infringement Types
- **Direct infringement (35 U.S.C. 271(a))**: Making, using, selling, offering to sell, or importing the patented invention without authorization. Strict liability; no intent required.
- **Induced infringement (35 U.S.C. 271(b))**: Actively encouraging another to infringe. Requires knowledge of the patent and intent to induce infringement.
- **Contributory infringement (35 U.S.C. 271(c))**: Selling a component with no substantial non-infringing use, knowing it is especially made for infringing use.
- **Doctrine of Equivalents (DOE)**: Infringement found when an element performs substantially the same function, in substantially the same way, to achieve substantially the same result. Limited by prosecution history estoppel and the all-elements rule.
- **Literal infringement**: Every element of a claim is found in the accused product/process.

### Design Around Strategies
- Identify the specific claim limitations that capture the accused product.
- Modify the product to avoid at least one element of each independent claim.
- Document the design-around effort (supports non-willfulness if later sued).
- Consider whether the doctrine of equivalents might still capture the modified design.
- File new patent applications on the design-around solution if novel.

### Patent Portfolio Strategy
- **Offensive**: Build a portfolio to assert against competitors, generate licensing revenue, establish market exclusivity, and increase company valuation.
- **Defensive**: Accumulate patents to deter litigation (mutual assured destruction), join defensive patent pools (e.g., LOT Network, Open Invention Network), publish defensively to create prior art.
- **Portfolio management**: Regular audits to prune low-value patents, identify white space for new filings, align portfolio with business strategy, and manage maintenance fee payments.

## 2. Trademark Law

### Distinctiveness Spectrum (Strongest to Weakest)
1. **Fanciful**: Coined words with no dictionary meaning (e.g., XEROX, KODAK). Inherently distinctive.
2. **Arbitrary**: Real words with no relation to the goods/services (e.g., APPLE for computers). Inherently distinctive.
3. **Suggestive**: Suggests a quality or characteristic but requires imagination to connect to the product (e.g., COPPERTONE for sunscreen). Inherently distinctive.
4. **Descriptive**: Directly describes a feature, quality, or characteristic of the goods/services (e.g., SHARP for televisions). Protectable only with proof of secondary meaning (acquired distinctiveness through use, advertising, consumer surveys).
5. **Generic**: The common name for the product/service (e.g., "escalator" for moving stairs). Never protectable. Marks can become generic through "genericide" (e.g., aspirin, thermos).

### Federal Registration (USPTO)
- **Principal Register**: Full benefits -- nationwide constructive notice, presumption of validity, incontestability after 5 years, customs recordation, use of (R) symbol, basis for international registration.
- **Supplemental Register**: For marks not yet distinctive enough for Principal Register. Provides ability to use (R) symbol, listed in USPTO search results (blocking similar applications), and basis for foreign registration. No presumption of validity or constructive notice.
- **Use-based application (Section 1(a))**: Filed when the mark is already in use in interstate commerce. Requires specimen showing use.
- **Intent-to-use (ITU) application (Section 1(b))**: Filed before use begins. Must file Statement of Use (SOU) with specimen before registration issues. Up to 36 months of extensions (6-month increments) after Notice of Allowance.

### Common Law Trademark Rights
- Rights arise from actual use in commerce, without registration.
- Geographic scope limited to the area of actual use and natural zone of expansion.
- Senior user in a geographic area has priority over a later federal registrant in that area (Tea Rose-Rectanus doctrine).
- Harder to enforce than registered marks; must prove use, priority, and likelihood of confusion.

### Likelihood of Confusion Analysis
**DuPont factors (TTAB/Federal Circuit):**
1. Similarity of marks (sight, sound, meaning, commercial impression)
2. Similarity of goods/services
3. Similarity of trade channels
4. Conditions of purchase (sophistication of buyers, cost of goods)
5. Fame of the prior mark
6. Number and nature of similar marks in use
7. Nature and extent of actual confusion
8. Length of concurrent use without confusion
9. Variety of goods on which the mark is used
10. Market interface between parties
11. Applicant's right to exclude others
12. Extent of potential confusion
13. Any other probative facts

**Sleekcraft factors (Ninth Circuit):** Similar analysis with 8 factors: strength of mark, proximity of goods, similarity of marks, evidence of actual confusion, marketing channels, type of goods and purchaser care, defendant's intent, likelihood of expansion.

### Trademark Infringement, Dilution, and Cybersquatting
- **Infringement**: Unauthorized use of a mark (or confusingly similar mark) in commerce in connection with goods/services where confusion is likely. Remedies: injunction, damages, profits, attorney's fees (exceptional cases), treble damages.
- **Dilution by blurring**: Association arising from similarity that impairs the distinctiveness of a famous mark. No confusion required. Mark must be famous (widely recognized by the general consuming public).
- **Dilution by tarnishment**: Association that harms the reputation of a famous mark (e.g., unwholesome or degrading use).
- **Cybersquatting (ACPA, 15 U.S.C. 1125(d))**: Bad-faith registration, trafficking in, or use of a domain name identical or confusingly similar to a distinctive or famous mark. Statutory damages up to $100,000 per domain. In rem jurisdiction available when registrant cannot be found.

### Domain Name Disputes (UDRP)
- Administered by WIPO or other approved providers under ICANN policy.
- Three elements: (1) domain is identical or confusingly similar to complainant's mark, (2) registrant has no rights or legitimate interests, (3) domain registered and used in bad faith.
- Remedies: transfer or cancellation of domain. No monetary damages.
- Decision typically within 60 days. Appeal only via court action.

### Trademark Clearance Search
- Start with USPTO TESS database (identical and phonetic equivalents).
- Expand to state trademark databases, common law use (internet, business directories, social media).
- Professional comprehensive search recommended before adoption and filing.
- Analyze results for likelihood of confusion, not just identical matches.
- Document clearance process for good faith defense.

### International Considerations
- **Madrid Protocol**: File international applications through USPTO designating member countries. Single application, single set of fees, centralized management. Dependent on home registration for first 5 years ("central attack" risk).
- **Paris Convention**: 6-month priority period from first filing in a member country.
- **Nice Classification**: Goods and services classified in 45 classes (34 goods, 11 services). Must register in each relevant class.

### Trademark Maintenance
- **Section 8 Declaration of Use**: Filed between 5th and 6th year after registration (6-month grace period). Must show continued use or excusable non-use. Failure to file results in cancellation.
- **Section 9 Renewal**: Filed every 10 years. Combined with Section 8 declaration.
- **Section 15 Declaration of Incontestability**: Filed after 5 consecutive years of use post-registration. Limits grounds for challenge (cannot be challenged on descriptiveness, prior use, etc.; still challengeable for genericism, fraud, abandonment, functionality).

### Trade Dress Protection
- Protects the overall commercial image or look and feel of a product or its packaging.
- Product packaging trade dress can be inherently distinctive. Product design trade dress requires secondary meaning (Wal-Mart v. Samara Bros.).
- Must be non-functional (both utilitarian and aesthetic functionality doctrines).
- Registered or unregistered (Section 43(a) of the Lanham Act).

## 3. Copyright Law

### Protectable Subject Matter
Original works of authorship fixed in a tangible medium of expression. Categories under 17 U.S.C. 102: literary works, musical works, dramatic works, pantomimes and choreography, pictorial/graphic/sculptural works, motion pictures and audiovisual works, sound recordings, architectural works. Facts and ideas are not copyrightable (idea-expression dichotomy). Minimal creativity required (Feist v. Rural Telephone).

### Copyright Ownership
- **Default**: Author is the initial owner. Joint authors share equal undivided ownership.
- **Work for hire (17 U.S.C. 101)**: Employer owns copyright in two situations:
  - (1) Work prepared by an employee within the scope of employment (use Reid factors to determine employee status: control, skill, tools, location, benefits, tax treatment, etc.).
  - (2) Work specially ordered or commissioned if (a) the parties expressly agree in a signed written instrument that it is a work for hire AND (b) the work falls within one of 9 statutory categories (contribution to collective work, part of a motion picture, translation, supplementary work, compilation, instructional text, test, test answers, atlas).
- If neither work-for-hire path applies, the hiring party must obtain a written assignment.

### Copyright Registration
- Registration is not required for copyright protection but is required before filing an infringement suit (for U.S. works).
- Registration within 3 months of publication (or before infringement begins) enables recovery of statutory damages ($750-$30,000 per work; up to $150,000 for willful infringement) and attorney's fees.
- Registration creates a presumption of validity if made within 5 years of first publication.
- Electronic filing through eCO system; registration effective on the date the Copyright Office receives the complete application.

### Fair Use (17 U.S.C. 107)
Four-factor test (all factors weighed together, no single factor dispositive):
1. **Purpose and character of the use**: Commercial vs nonprofit/educational. Transformative use weighs heavily in favor (Campbell v. Acuff-Rose). After Andy Warhol Foundation v. Goldsmith (2023), transformation requires a fundamentally different purpose or character, not merely a new aesthetic.
2. **Nature of the copyrighted work**: Creative works get more protection than factual works. Published works more susceptible to fair use than unpublished.
3. **Amount and substantiality used**: Both quantitative and qualitative. Using the "heart" of the work weighs against fair use even if a small portion.
4. **Effect on the market**: Considers harm to the existing market and potential licensing markets. After Google v. Oracle (2021), a use that serves a different market may weigh in favor.

### DMCA (Digital Millennium Copyright Act)
- **Safe harbors (Section 512)**: ISPs/platforms protected from liability for user-uploaded content if they: (1) designate a DMCA agent with the Copyright Office, (2) adopt and implement a repeat infringer policy, (3) do not have actual knowledge of infringement, (4) act expeditiously to remove upon notification, (5) do not receive direct financial benefit from infringing activity they can control.
- **Takedown/counter-notice**: Copyright owner sends takedown notice (Section 512(c)(3)); service provider removes content; alleged infringer may file counter-notification; content restored in 10-14 business days unless copyright owner files suit.
- **Anti-circumvention (Section 1201)**: Prohibits circumventing technological protection measures (TPMs) controlling access to copyrighted works. Separate from infringement -- circumvention is independently actionable. Exemptions reviewed triennially by Librarian of Congress.

### AI and Copyright (Current State of Law)
- **AI-generated works**: The Copyright Office has stated that works generated entirely by AI without human creative control are not copyrightable (Thaler v. Perlmutter, 2023). Works with sufficient human authorship in selection, arrangement, or modification may qualify.
- **Training data**: Open litigation (e.g., NYT v. OpenAI, Getty v. Stability AI) on whether training AI models on copyrighted works constitutes fair use. No definitive ruling yet.
- **Registration guidance**: Copyright Office requires disclosure of AI-generated content in applications. Human-authored portions may be registered; purely AI-generated portions must be disclaimed.

### Duration
- Works created on or after January 1, 1978: life of the author plus 70 years.
- Joint works: life of the last surviving author plus 70 years.
- Works for hire, anonymous, pseudonymous: 95 years from publication or 120 years from creation, whichever is shorter.
- Pre-1978 works: complex rules depending on registration, renewal, and publication status.

### Assignment and Licensing
- **Assignment**: Transfer of copyright ownership. Must be in writing and signed by the owner (17 U.S.C. 204(a)).
- **Exclusive license**: Grants exclusive rights in one or more of the Section 106 rights. Must be in writing. Exclusive licensee has standing to sue.
- **Non-exclusive license**: Can be oral or implied. Licensee generally lacks standing to sue for infringement.
- **Termination of transfers**: Authors (or heirs) may terminate grants made on or after January 1, 1978, after 35 years (Section 203). Pre-1978 grants terminable after 56 years (Section 304).

## 4. Trade Secrets

### Federal: Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA, 2016)
- Creates a federal private right of action for trade secret misappropriation.
- Ex parte seizure available in extraordinary circumstances (applicant must show other equitable relief is inadequate).
- Whistleblower immunity: employees who disclose trade secrets to government officials or in sealed court filings for reporting suspected violations of law are immune from liability. Employers must provide notice of this immunity in confidentiality agreements (or face inability to recover exemplary damages/attorney's fees).
- Remedies: injunction (limited to what is necessary, cannot exceed the period the secret would have remained secret, cannot prevent employment unless based on evidence of threatened misappropriation), damages (actual loss + unjust enrichment, or reasonable royalty), exemplary damages up to 2x for willful and malicious misappropriation, attorney's fees.

### State: Uniform Trade Secrets Act (UTSA)
- Adopted in 48 states plus DC and USVI. Notable holdouts: New York (uses common law) and North Carolina (has its own statute).
- Definitions and remedies vary by state adoption. Some states require clear and convincing evidence; others require preponderance.
- Many states allow both DTSA and state law claims simultaneously.

### Trade Secret Requirements
1. **Derives independent economic value** (actual or potential) from not being generally known to or readily ascertainable by others who can obtain economic value from its disclosure or use.
2. **Reasonable efforts to maintain secrecy**: The owner must take affirmative steps to protect the information.

### Reasonable Measures to Protect Secrecy
- **NDAs/Confidentiality agreements**: With employees, contractors, vendors, partners, potential acquirers.
- **Access controls**: Need-to-know basis, role-based access, password protection, encryption.
- **Employee measures**: Onboarding training, exit interviews, reminders of obligations, restrictive covenant agreements.
- **Physical security**: Locked facilities, visitor logs, clean desk policies.
- **Digital security**: Network security, access logging, DLP software, encrypted communications.
- **Labeling**: Mark documents and materials as "Confidential" or "Trade Secret."
- **Contractual protections**: Assignment and non-disclosure provisions in employment and vendor agreements.
- **Periodic audits**: Review and update protection measures regularly.

### Misappropriation
- **Improper means**: Theft, bribery, misrepresentation, breach or inducement of a breach of duty to maintain secrecy, espionage (including electronic).
- **Acquisition**: Acquiring a trade secret by improper means.
- **Disclosure/Use**: Disclosing or using a trade secret obtained by improper means, or in breach of a duty of confidence.
- **Reverse engineering and independent discovery** are NOT improper means (key distinction from patent law).

### Inevitable Disclosure Doctrine
- Some courts enjoin a former employee from working for a competitor when they would inevitably use or disclose trade secrets, even without a non-compete.
- Narrow application and not recognized in all jurisdictions. Illinois legislatively abolished it. California courts generally reject it.
- Requires showing that the new position would inevitably lead to disclosure, not merely that the employee possesses trade secrets.

### Trade Secret vs. Patent Strategy
- **Mutually exclusive** for the same information: a patent publishes the invention (destroying secrecy); a trade secret requires non-disclosure.
- **Favor trade secrets when**: The secret is not independently discoverable or reverse-engineerable, the competitive advantage is in the process rather than the product, the information has indefinite useful life, patent prosecution costs are prohibitive.
- **Favor patents when**: The innovation is independently discoverable or reverse-engineerable, you need offensive enforcement capability, the patent term (20 years) is sufficient, you need to disclose the technology to investors/partners.

### Trade Secret Audit Checklist
- [ ] Identify and inventory all trade secrets (formulas, algorithms, customer lists, business strategies, manufacturing processes, pricing models)
- [ ] Classify by value and sensitivity level
- [ ] Review all NDAs and confidentiality agreements for adequacy
- [ ] Audit access controls (physical and digital)
- [ ] Review employee onboarding and offboarding procedures
- [ ] Assess vendor and third-party access and protections
- [ ] Test and document security measures
- [ ] Review and update labeling/marking practices
- [ ] Evaluate trade secret insurance coverage
- [ ] Document the audit itself (evidence of reasonable measures)

## 5. IP Assignment & Licensing

### IP Assignment in Employment
- **Present assignment ("hereby assigns")**: Automatically transfers ownership at the moment of creation. Preferred language for employers. Self-executing.
- **Obligation to assign ("agrees to assign")**: Creates a contractual promise, but title may not transfer until a separate assignment document is executed. Can create issues in bankruptcy or if the employee refuses.
- Best practice: use "hereby assigns" plus a backup obligation to execute confirmatory documents.

### Prior Inventions Carve-Out
- **California (Labor Code 2870)**: Employers cannot require assignment of inventions developed entirely on the employee's own time, without employer equipment, that do not relate to the employer's business or anticipated R&D, and do not result from work performed for the employer. Employee must disclose and employer must provide written acknowledgment.
- **Similar statutes**: Delaware (19 Del. C. 805), Illinois (765 ILCS 1060/2), Minnesota (Minn. Stat. 181.78), Washington (RCW 49.44.140), and others.
- Best practice: include a schedule of prior inventions in the employment agreement; employee lists existing IP to carve out.

### Scope of Assignment
- Typical scope: inventions conceived or reduced to practice (1) during employment, (2) using company resources or information, (3) related to the company's current or anticipated business.
- Overly broad provisions may be unenforceable (state-dependent).
- Should cover all forms of IP: patents, copyrights, trade secrets, mask works, moral rights (to the extent assignable).

### Work-for-Hire vs. Assignment
- **Work for hire (copyright only)**: Employer is the "author" from inception. No termination right under Section 203.
- **Assignment (patents, trade secrets, other IP)**: Original owner transfers rights. Patent assignments should be recorded with the USPTO. Trade secret ownership transfers via contract.
- For independent contractors: work-for-hire status is limited to the 9 statutory categories plus a signed agreement. Always pair with an express assignment clause as backup.

### License Types
- **Exclusive license**: Only the licensee may exercise the licensed rights (even the licensor is excluded unless otherwise agreed). Must be in writing for copyright. Exclusive licensee has standing to sue infringers.
- **Non-exclusive license**: Multiple licensees may exercise the same rights. Licensor retains the right to use and license to others. Oral grant sufficient for copyright (though written is best practice).
- **Sole license**: Only one licensee, but the licensor retains the right to use. A middle ground between exclusive and non-exclusive.

### License Terms and Restrictions
- **Field of use**: Limits the licensee to specified applications or markets.
- **Territory**: Geographic restrictions on where the licensee can exercise rights.
- **Sublicensing**: Right to sublicense should be explicitly granted or withheld. Address sublicensee obligations and survival on termination.
- **Duration**: Fixed term, perpetual, or tied to the life of the underlying IP.

### Royalty Structures
- **Running royalty**: Percentage of net sales or per-unit fee. Requires audit rights and reporting obligations.
- **Lump sum**: One-time payment. Simple but may undervalue long-term IP.
- **Minimum guarantee / minimum annual royalty**: Ensures baseline payment regardless of sales. Often combined with running royalty.
- **Milestone payments**: Triggered by development or commercial milestones (regulatory approval, first commercial sale, revenue thresholds).
- **Most-favored-licensee clause**: Guarantees the licensee will receive terms no less favorable than those offered to future licensees.

### IP in M&A
- Representations and warranties: ownership, non-infringement, no encumbrances, validity, absence of claims.
- IP due diligence: chain of title, employee/contractor assignments, third-party licenses, open source exposure, litigation history.
- Assignment provisions in the purchase agreement: ensure all IP transfers cleanly at closing.
- Post-closing obligations: further assurances, cooperation in prosecution, employee transition.

## 6. Open Source Software

### License Categories
- **Permissive (MIT, BSD 2-Clause, BSD 3-Clause, Apache 2.0)**: Allow use, modification, and redistribution with minimal restrictions (typically attribution only). Apache 2.0 includes an express patent license grant and patent retaliation clause.
- **Weak copyleft (LGPL, MPL 2.0, EPL)**: Modifications to the licensed component must be shared under the same license, but larger works incorporating the component need not be. LGPL common for libraries.
- **Strong copyleft (GPL v2, GPL v3, AGPL v3)**: Derivative works must be distributed under the same license with source code. GPL v3 adds patent and anti-tivoization provisions. AGPL extends copyleft to server-side use (if users interact over a network, source must be provided).

### Copyleft Obligations
- **Source code disclosure**: Must provide (or offer to provide) complete corresponding source code when distributing copyleft-licensed binaries.
- **Derivative works**: Any work "based on" the copyleft code inherits the license. Definition of "derivative work" is debated (linking, dynamic vs. static, separate process communication).
- **Distribution trigger**: Obligations activate upon "distribution" (or "conveying" under GPL v3). Internal use typically does not trigger. AGPL uniquely triggers on network interaction.

### AGPL Considerations for SaaS
- AGPL was specifically designed to close the "SaaS loophole" in GPL.
- If AGPL code is modified and users interact with it over a network, the complete source (including modifications) must be made available.
- Many companies have blanket policies against using AGPL-licensed code in their products.
- Consider whether AGPL components can be isolated or replaced with permissive alternatives.

### License Compatibility
- MIT/BSD are compatible with virtually all other licenses (can be incorporated into GPL projects).
- Apache 2.0 is compatible with GPL v3 but not GPL v2 (due to patent clause conflicts).
- GPL v2 and GPL v3 are not compatible with each other unless the code is licensed "GPL v2 or later."
- Copyleft licenses generally cannot be combined with each other (GPL + MPL, GPL + EPL) without dual-licensing or specific compatibility provisions.

### Open Source Compliance Program
- Maintain an inventory of all open source components used (software bill of materials / SBOM).
- Track license obligations for each component.
- Implement review/approval process before introducing new open source dependencies.
- Ensure compliance with attribution, notice, and source code obligations.
- Train developers on open source policies and risks.
- Use automated scanning tools (e.g., FOSSA, Snyk, Black Duck, ScanCode) to detect open source in codebases.

### Contribution Licensing
- **Contributor License Agreement (CLA)**: Contributor grants the project broad rights (often including patent license and right to relicense). Can be individual or corporate. Required by Apache Foundation, Google, Microsoft, others.
- **Developer Certificate of Origin (DCO)**: Lighter-weight alternative. Contributor certifies they have the right to submit the contribution and it is submitted under the project's license. Used by Linux kernel, GitLab, others. Implemented via `Signed-off-by` line in commits.

### Open Source Due Diligence in M&A
- Full audit of all open source usage in target's products and internal tools.
- Identify copyleft exposure (especially AGPL and GPL in proprietary products).
- Review compliance with license terms (attribution, source availability, notices).
- Assess risk of undisclosed open source (scanning with SCA tools).
- Evaluate target's open source policies and governance.
- Check for contributions to open source projects (may include patent grants via CLA).

## 7. IP Due Diligence Checklist

### Patent Portfolio
- [ ] List all issued patents (jurisdiction, filing date, issue date, expiration date, maintenance status)
- [ ] List all pending applications (provisional, non-provisional, PCT, foreign)
- [ ] Confirm chain of title (inventor assignments recorded with USPTO/foreign offices)
- [ ] Review prosecution history for any disclaimers or narrowing amendments
- [ ] Identify any reexamination, IPR, PGR, or opposition proceedings
- [ ] Assess patent landscape and competitive coverage
- [ ] Review any patent licenses granted or received

### Trademark Portfolio
- [ ] List all registered trademarks (USPTO, state, international) with status and renewal dates
- [ ] List all pending applications (basis, status, office actions)
- [ ] Identify common law marks in use but not registered
- [ ] Review domain name portfolio and social media handles
- [ ] Check for any oppositions, cancellations, or disputes
- [ ] Verify Section 8/9/15 filings are current
- [ ] Assess strength and distinctiveness of key marks

### Copyright
- [ ] Identify key copyrightable works (software, content, documentation, marketing materials)
- [ ] Verify ownership chain (work-for-hire analysis for each significant work)
- [ ] Review all employee and contractor IP assignment agreements
- [ ] Check copyright registrations (if any)
- [ ] Identify any third-party content incorporated under license
- [ ] Review any copyright licenses granted

### Trade Secrets
- [ ] Inventory key trade secrets by category and business value
- [ ] Audit protection measures (NDAs, access controls, security, labeling)
- [ ] Review employee confidentiality and invention assignment agreements
- [ ] Assess departing employee procedures (exit interviews, device return, access revocation)
- [ ] Check for any known or suspected misappropriation incidents
- [ ] Review trade secret insurance coverage

### Open Source
- [ ] Complete software composition analysis (SCA) scan of all products
- [ ] Identify all open source components and their licenses
- [ ] Assess copyleft exposure (GPL, AGPL, LGPL in proprietary code)
- [ ] Verify compliance with attribution and notice requirements
- [ ] Review open source contribution policies and CLA/DCO compliance
- [ ] Identify any open source components with known vulnerabilities

### Third-Party IP
- [ ] Inventory all inbound IP licenses (software, content, data, technology)
- [ ] Review license terms, restrictions, and transferability (change-of-control provisions)
- [ ] Identify any licenses that require consent for assignment in M&A
- [ ] Assess ongoing royalty obligations and payment history
- [ ] Review any indemnification provisions

### Litigation and Disputes
- [ ] List all pending or threatened IP litigation (plaintiff and defendant matters)
- [ ] Review any past IP litigation and outcomes
- [ ] Identify any cease-and-desist letters sent or received
- [ ] Review any FTO opinions obtained
- [ ] Assess IP insurance coverage and claims history

### Employee/Contractor Audit
- [ ] Verify all current employees have signed IP assignment agreements
- [ ] Verify all current contractors have signed IP assignment and work-for-hire agreements
- [ ] Review prior inventions schedules for key technical employees
- [ ] Identify any employees subject to restrictive covenants from prior employers
- [ ] Review any consulting or advisory agreements with IP provisions
