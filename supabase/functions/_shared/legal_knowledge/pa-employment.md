## 1. At-Will Employment

### Default Rule
Employment is presumed at-will in 49 states (plus DC). Either employer or employee may terminate the relationship at any time, for any reason not prohibited by law, with or without notice.

### Exceptions to At-Will

**Implied Contract Exception** (recognized in ~38 states)
- Employee handbooks stating progressive discipline or "for cause" termination
- Oral promises of continued employment or job security
- Course of dealing suggesting termination only for cause
- Key defense: Clear, conspicuous handbook disclaimers preserving at-will status

**Public Policy Exception** (recognized in ~44 states)
- Termination for refusing to commit an illegal act
- Termination for exercising a statutory right (filing workers' comp claim, voting, jury duty)
- Termination for reporting illegal conduct (whistleblowing)
- Termination for performing a public obligation
- Sources vary: statute only (narrow) vs. constitution, regulations, judicial decisions (broad)

**Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing** (recognized in ~11 states)
- Most limited exception; primarily Alaska, Arizona, California, Delaware, Idaho, Massachusetts, Montana, Nevada, Utah, Wyoming
- Cannot terminate to deprive employee of earned benefits (e.g., commissions, vesting)

**Montana — Wrongful Discharge from Employment Act (WDEA)**
- Only state that is not at-will after probationary period
- After probation (or 1 year if no probationary period defined), termination must be for good cause
- Good cause: reasonable job-related grounds for dismissal based on failure to perform, disruption of operations, or other legitimate business reason
- Caps damages at 4 years of lost wages

## 2. Restrictive Covenants

### Non-Compete Agreements

**FTC Final Rule (2024) — Current Status**
- FTC issued final rule banning most non-competes (effective date set for Sep 4, 2024)
- **Enjoined nationwide** by federal court in Ryan LLC v. FTC (N.D. Tex., Aug 2024)
- FTC appealed; as of 2026, rule remains unenforceable pending litigation
- Practical effect: Non-competes still governed by state law; advise clients on state-specific enforceability

**Common Enforceability Factors**
- Protectable interest (trade secrets, customer relationships, specialized training, goodwill)
- Reasonable scope: Duration (6 months to 2 years typical), Geographic area (tied to actual business territory), Activity restriction (limited to competing activities)
- Adequate consideration (initial employment, continued employment, additional consideration depending on state)
- Not unduly burdensome on employee or harmful to public interest

**Blue-Pencil vs. Red-Pencil States**
- **Blue-pencil** (majority): Court modifies overbroad terms to make enforceable (TX, FL, GA, NC, MI, OH, IL, and others)
- **Red-pencil** (minority): Overbroad covenant is entirely void; court will not rewrite (NE, WI, VA traditionally)
- **Reformation**: Some states allow complete judicial rewriting (TX, MA)

**Garden Leave Provisions**
- Employer pays salary during restricted period; employee does not work
- Increasingly required or favored in states like Massachusetts, Oregon, Illinois
- Strengthens enforceability by reducing hardship

**State-Specific Bans and Restrictions**
| State | Rule |
|-------|------|
| California | Complete ban (Bus. & Prof. Code 16600); void and unenforceable regardless of where signed; employer must notify former employees of voidness |
| Oklahoma | Generally bans non-competes; limited exception for sale of business |
| North Dakota | Generally bans non-competes; limited exceptions |
| Minnesota | Banned effective Jul 1, 2023 (prospective only) |
| Colorado | Prohibited for workers earning below 60th percentile of state average wage; allowed for highly compensated with notice requirements |
| Illinois | Prohibited for workers earning below $75K/year (non-compete) or $45K/year (non-solicit); requires 14-day notice and advise-counsel provision |
| Oregon | Limited to 1 year; employee must earn above median income; garden leave or enforceable only if employer provides signed copy at offer or 2 weeks before termination |
| Massachusetts | Limited to 1 year; garden leave or other mutually agreed consideration required; prohibited for non-exempt employees, 18+, students, laid-off workers |
| Washington | Prohibited for workers earning below $116,593/year (employees) or $291,483 (independent contractors); limited to 18 months |
| Maine | Prohibited for workers earning 400% or less of federal poverty level |
| Maryland | Prohibited for workers earning below $19.88/hr or $41,340/yr |
| Virginia | Prohibited for low-wage workers (earning less than average weekly wage) |
| Rhode Island | Prohibited for workers earning 250% or less of state minimum wage, students, interns, under-18, non-exempt |
| DC | Near-total ban with limited exceptions (medical specialists, highly compensated) |

### Non-Solicitation Agreements

**Customer Non-Solicitation** — Generally more enforceable than non-competes
- Must be limited to customers with whom employee had material contact
- Reasonable duration (typically 12-24 months)
- Cannot bar passive acceptance of business from former customers (in most states)

**Employee Non-Solicitation** — Moderately enforceable
- Restriction on recruiting former colleagues
- Must be reasonable in scope and duration
- Some states scrutinize as de facto non-competes (California — largely unenforceable)

### Confidentiality / NDA Agreements
- Must reasonably define "confidential information" — cannot be catch-all
- Standard carve-outs: information in the public domain, independently developed, known prior to employment, received from third party without restriction
- Term: Often indefinite for trade secrets; 2-5 years typical for general confidential information
- Survival: Obligations typically survive termination
- Cannot restrict use of general skills, knowledge, and experience

### Non-Disparagement Clauses
- **NLRA Section 7**: Cannot restrict employees from discussing terms and conditions of employment or engaging in protected concerted activity (applies to non-supervisory employees)
- **SEC Rule 21F-17**: Cannot impede whistleblowing to the SEC
- **SPEAK OUT Act (2022)**: Pre-dispute non-disparagement clauses unenforceable for claims of sexual harassment or sexual assault
- **Enforceability**: Generally enforceable if mutual and reasonable; increasingly scrutinized

## 3. Worker Classification

### IRS 20-Factor Test (Common Law) — Three Categories
**Behavioral Control**: Does the company control how the worker performs tasks?
- Instructions on when, where, how to work; training provided; tools/equipment provided
**Financial Control**: Business aspects of the worker's role
- Significant investment; unreimbursed expenses; opportunity for profit/loss; services available to market; method of payment
**Relationship Type**: How parties perceive the relationship
- Written contracts; employee benefits; permanency; integral to business

### DOL Economic Reality Test (FLSA) — Six Factors
1. Extent to which work is integral to the employer's business
2. Worker's opportunity for profit or loss depending on managerial skill
3. Extent of relative investments of employer and worker
4. Whether work requires special skills and initiative
5. Permanency of the relationship
6. Degree of control exercised or retained by employer
- 2024 DOL Final Rule: Restored multi-factor totality-of-the-circumstances test (replacing 2021 rule focusing on control and profit opportunity)

### ABC Test (California AB5 and Expanding)
Worker is an employee unless the hiring entity proves ALL three:
- **A**: Worker is free from the control and direction of the hiring entity (contract and fact)
- **B**: Worker performs work outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business
- **C**: Worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business of the same nature

States using ABC test (in some form): California, Connecticut, Delaware, Illinois, Indiana, Massachusetts, Nebraska, New Hampshire, New Jersey, Vermont, Washington (for some purposes)

### Misclassification Consequences
- Back employment taxes (employer share of FICA) plus penalties and interest
- Back wages, overtime, minimum wage (FLSA and state law)
- Benefits liability (health insurance, retirement, workers' compensation)
- State unemployment tax liability
- Class/collective action exposure (potentially thousands of workers)
- Personal liability for corporate officers in some jurisdictions

## 4. Wage and Hour (FLSA)

### Exempt vs. Non-Exempt

**Salary Basis Test**: Must be paid a predetermined fixed salary not subject to reduction based on quality/quantity of work
- Current threshold: $844/week ($43,888/year) as of Jul 1, 2024
- Note: DOL's 2024 rule attempted to raise to $1,128/week ($58,656/year) effective Jan 1, 2025 — this increase was struck down by federal court in Nov 2024; the $844 threshold from Jul 2024 remains in effect
- Highly Compensated Employee (HCE): $107,432/year total compensation (2024 level; higher threshold also blocked)

**Duties Tests**
| Exemption | Primary Duty |
|-----------|-------------|
| Executive | Managing the enterprise or a recognized department; directs 2+ employees; authority to hire/fire or recommend |
| Administrative | Office/non-manual work directly related to management or business operations; exercise of discretion and independent judgment on significant matters |
| Professional (Learned) | Work requiring advanced knowledge in a field of science or learning, customarily acquired by prolonged specialized instruction |
| Professional (Creative) | Work requiring invention, imagination, originality, or talent in a recognized creative field |
| Computer Employee | Systems analyst, programmer, software engineer; $684/week salary or $27.63/hour |
| Outside Sales | Primary duty of making sales or obtaining orders; customarily and regularly engaged away from employer's place of business |

### State Variations Exceeding FLSA
- **California**: Daily overtime (over 8 hours/day and over 12 hours/day); double time after 12 hours; 7th consecutive day overtime; higher salary threshold for exemption (2x state minimum wage)
- **New York**: Higher salary thresholds for exempt employees ($1,200/week in NYC, varying downstate/upstate)
- **Washington**: Salary threshold tied to state minimum wage multiplier (1.75x for small employers, 2x for large)
- **Colorado**: Daily overtime threshold (12 hours/day); unique rest period requirements

### Common Violations
- **Off-the-clock work**: Pre-shift/post-shift tasks, email monitoring, donning/doffing
- **Meal/rest break violations**: Auto-deducting meal breaks, not providing required breaks (CA: 30-min meal by 5th hour, 10-min rest per 4 hours)
- **Tip credit issues**: Improper tip pooling, failure to notify, tip credit below minimum
- **Misclassification as exempt**: Applying exemption without meeting duties test
- **Rounding practices**: Must be neutral over time; systematic rounding down is unlawful
- **Intern/trainee classification**: DOL "primary beneficiary" test (7 factors)

## 5. Discrimination and Harassment

### Federal Protected Classes
| Statute | Protected Class | Employer Size |
|---------|----------------|---------------|
| Title VII | Race, color, religion, sex, national origin | 15+ employees |
| ADA/ADAAA | Disability (physical or mental) | 15+ employees |
| ADEA | Age (40 and older) | 20+ employees |
| GINA | Genetic information | 15+ employees |
| PDA | Pregnancy, childbirth, related conditions | 15+ employees |
| Equal Pay Act | Sex-based wage disparities | All employers |
| Section 1981 | Race and ethnicity (broader than Title VII) | All employers, no admin exhaustion required |

**Title VII Expanded — Bostock v. Clayton County (2020)**: Discrimination "because of sex" includes sexual orientation and gender identity

### ADA Reasonable Accommodation
- **Interactive process**: Employer and employee must engage in good-faith dialogue
- **Examples**: Modified work schedule, telework, reassignment, equipment modification, leave of absence, job restructuring
- **Undue hardship defense**: Significant difficulty or expense considering employer's size, resources, and nature of operations
- **Essential functions**: Accommodation not required if employee cannot perform essential job functions even with accommodation
- **Direct threat**: Can deny accommodation if employee poses significant risk of substantial harm

### Harassment Standards
- **Severe or pervasive**: Conduct must be severe OR pervasive enough to create hostile work environment (single severe incident can suffice)
- **Employer liability (supervisors)**: Automatic liability if tangible employment action; otherwise Faragher-Ellerth affirmative defense (reasonable care to prevent/correct; employee unreasonably failed to use preventive mechanisms)
- **Employer liability (coworkers)**: Negligence standard — knew or should have known and failed to take prompt remedial action
- **Quid pro quo**: Supervisor conditions employment benefit on submission to sexual advances

### Retaliation
- Most common charge filed with EEOC (>50% of all charges)
- Protected activity: Filing a charge, participating in investigation, opposing discriminatory practice
- Standard: Would the action dissuade a reasonable worker from engaging in protected activity? (Burlington Northern)
- Close temporal proximity is relevant but usually insufficient alone for causation

## 6. Leave Laws

### FMLA (Family and Medical Leave Act)
**Eligibility**: 50+ employees within 75 miles; employee has worked 12+ months AND 1,250+ hours in preceding 12 months
**Qualifying Reasons**:
- Birth/placement of child (bonding leave — within 12 months)
- Serious health condition of employee
- Serious health condition of spouse, child, or parent
- Qualifying exigency related to military service of family member
- Military caregiver leave (26 weeks for serious injury/illness of covered service member)

**Key Provisions**: 12 weeks unpaid in 12-month period; intermittent leave permitted for medical necessity; equivalent position upon return; continuation of health benefits; no retaliation

### State Paid Leave Programs (selected)
- **California (SDI/PFL)**: Up to 8 weeks paid family leave; up to 52 weeks state disability
- **New York (PFL)**: Up to 12 weeks at 67% AWW (capped)
- **Washington (PFML)**: Up to 12 weeks family, 12 weeks medical, 16 weeks combined; up to 18 weeks with pregnancy complications
- **New Jersey (FLI/TDI)**: Up to 12 weeks family leave; up to 26 weeks disability
- **Massachusetts (PFML)**: Up to 12 weeks family, 20 weeks medical, 26 weeks combined
- **Colorado (FAMLI)**: Up to 12 weeks (16 with pregnancy complications) effective Jan 1, 2024
- **Oregon (PFML)**: Up to 12 weeks (14 with pregnancy complications) effective Sep 2023

### Other Leave Types
- **ADA Leave**: Leave as reasonable accommodation; no specific duration but must be finite and not indefinite
- **PWFA (Pregnant Workers Fairness Act, 2023)**: Reasonable accommodations for pregnancy, childbirth, related conditions; covers 15+ employees; similar framework to ADA
- **USERRA**: Up to 5 cumulative years of military leave; reemployment rights upon return; escalator principle (return to position would have held absent military service)
- **PUMP Act (2022)**: Break time and private space for nursing employees for up to 1 year after birth; covers nearly all employers; employees can file private lawsuits

## 7. Employment Agreements — Key Provisions

### Compensation Structure
- Base salary, payment frequency, exempt/non-exempt classification
- Bonus: Discretionary vs. non-discretionary; performance metrics; payment timing; pro-ration upon termination
- Commission: Plan document incorporation; draw provisions; post-termination commission rights (state law varies)
- Equity: Stock options (ISO vs. NSO), RSUs, profits interests; vesting schedule; acceleration triggers; exercise periods
- Benefits: Health insurance, retirement, PTO (reference to plan documents)

### IP Assignment (PIIA — Proprietary Information and Inventions Assignment)
- Assignment of all inventions conceived or reduced to practice during employment and related to employer's business or using employer resources
- **Prior inventions schedule**: Must allow employee to list pre-existing IP
- **State carve-outs**: California (Lab. Code 2870), Delaware, Illinois, Kansas, Minnesota, North Carolina, Washington — cannot require assignment of inventions developed entirely on employee's own time, without employer resources, unrelated to employer's business
- Work made for hire doctrine (copyright): Applies to works within scope of employment; for commissioned works, limited to 9 statutory categories with written agreement

### Termination Provisions
- **"Cause" definition**: Typically includes conviction of felony, material breach of agreement, willful misconduct, fraud, substance abuse, failure to perform duties after notice and cure
- **Cure periods**: 15-30 days typical for curable breaches
- **Good Reason** (constructive termination trigger): Material reduction in compensation, material diminution of duties, relocation beyond specified distance, material breach by employer
- **Severance triggers**: Termination without Cause; resignation for Good Reason; non-renewal by employer

### Change of Control Provisions
- **Single trigger**: Acceleration of equity/severance upon change of control alone
- **Double trigger**: Acceleration only upon change of control PLUS termination without cause or resignation for good reason within specified period (12-24 months)
- **280G considerations**: Excise tax on "excess parachute payments" exceeding 3x base amount; gross-up vs. best-of-net (cutback) provisions

### Dispute Resolution
- **Arbitration clauses**: Generally enforceable under FAA; class/collective action waivers permitted (Epic Systems v. Lewis)
- **Ending Forced Arbitration Act (2022)**: Pre-dispute arbitration agreements unenforceable for claims of sexual harassment or sexual assault (at claimant's election)
- **NLRA limitations**: Arbitration with class waiver generally enforceable for employment claims but not for NLRA Section 7 claims before the Board
- **Venue/choice of law**: Specify governing law and forum; consider enforceability if employee resides elsewhere

## 8. Separation and Severance

### OWBPA Requirements (Age 40+ Releases)
Under the Older Workers Benefit Protection Act, a release of ADEA claims must:
- Be written in plain language
- Specifically refer to ADEA rights or claims
- Not waive rights arising after the date of the agreement
- Provide consideration beyond what employee is already entitled to
- Advise employee in writing to consult an attorney
- Provide **21 days** to consider (individual termination) or **45 days** (group/RIF)
- Provide **7-day revocation period** after signing
- For group terminations: Disclose the decisional unit, eligibility factors, job titles and ages of all individuals eligible and selected/not selected

### General Release Requirements
- Must be knowing and voluntary
- Adequate consideration (something beyond what is already owed)
- Clear and unambiguous language
- Reasonable scope (overreaching releases may be void)
- Cannot waive: future claims, unemployment benefits, workers' compensation benefits, right to file EEOC charge (though can waive right to monetary recovery), OSHA rights, whistleblower protections under SOX/Dodd-Frank/False Claims Act

### WARN Act (Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act)
- **Federal WARN**: 60 days advance written notice for plant closings (50+ employees at single site) or mass layoffs (500+ employees or 50-499 if 33%+ of workforce at site)
- **Penalties**: Back pay and benefits for each day of violation, up to 60 days; $500/day civil penalty for failure to notify local government
- **Exceptions**: Faltering company, unforeseeable business circumstances, natural disaster
- **State Mini-WARN Laws**: California (Cal-WARN, 75+ employees, broader triggers), New York (also 75+, 90 days), New Jersey (100+, 90 days), Illinois, Maryland, Tennessee, Wisconsin (varying thresholds and notice periods)

### COBRA
- Employers with 20+ employees must offer continuation of group health coverage
- Qualifying events: Termination (voluntary or involuntary, except gross misconduct), reduction in hours, divorce/legal separation, death, Medicare entitlement, dependent aging out
- Duration: 18 months (termination/hours reduction), 36 months (other qualifying events)
- Notice: Employer must notify plan administrator within 30 days; administrator must notify qualified beneficiaries within 14 days
- Premium: Up to 102% of full premium cost (employer + employee share + 2% admin)

## 9. Employment Law Review Checklist

### Agreement Type Identification
- [ ] Employment agreement (offer letter vs. comprehensive agreement)
- [ ] Independent contractor agreement
- [ ] Restrictive covenant (standalone or within employment agreement)
- [ ] Separation/severance agreement
- [ ] Equity/compensation plan documents
- [ ] Confidentiality/invention assignment agreement

### Jurisdiction Determination
- [ ] Where does the employee physically work? (Primary determinant)
- [ ] Remote/multi-state workers: Where does work predominantly occur?
- [ ] State-specific requirements triggered by employee location, NOT employer HQ
- [ ] Choice-of-law provisions: Enforceable? Does local mandatory law override?

### Restrictive Covenant Enforceability Analysis
- [ ] Identify applicable state law (employee work location)
- [ ] Is the covenant type prohibited in this jurisdiction?
- [ ] Assess consideration (new employment, continued employment, additional consideration)
- [ ] Evaluate scope: duration, geography, activity restriction
- [ ] Identify protectable interest (trade secrets, customer relationships, specialized training)
- [ ] Blue-pencil or red-pencil state?
- [ ] Income/compensation threshold requirements met?
- [ ] Notice requirements satisfied?
- [ ] Garden leave required or provided?

### Classification Audit
- [ ] Identify applicable test (ABC, economic reality, common law)
- [ ] Assess behavioral control factors
- [ ] Assess financial control factors
- [ ] Assess relationship factors
- [ ] Review industry-specific exemptions
- [ ] Evaluate misclassification exposure (back wages, taxes, benefits, penalties)

### Compensation Compliance Check
- [ ] Correct exempt/non-exempt classification (salary basis + duties test)
- [ ] Salary meets federal AND state thresholds
- [ ] Overtime properly calculated (regular rate includes all non-discretionary compensation)
- [ ] Meal/rest break compliance (state-specific)
- [ ] Commission plan compliant with state requirements (written agreement, payment timing)
- [ ] Equity grants properly structured (409A compliance, securities law)
- [ ] Final pay timing compliant (varies by state — California: immediately upon involuntary termination, 72 hours upon voluntary; varies widely)

### Required Disclosures and Notices
- [ ] Offer letter/employment agreement: At-will disclaimer, compensation, start date, position
- [ ] Handbook acknowledgment with at-will disclaimer
- [ ] State-required wage theft notices (NY, CA, and others — written notice of pay rate, pay day, employer info)
- [ ] Non-compete notice requirements (IL: 14 days + advise counsel; MA: with offer or 10 business days before start; OR: with offer or 2 weeks before termination)
- [ ] Pay transparency requirements (CA, CO, CT, MD, NV, NY, WA — salary range in job postings and/or upon request)
- [ ] Separation: COBRA notice, state continuation, unemployment information, OWBPA disclosures if applicable
